Section 1.1 · The Hook

Your first board meeting

Your decision

Café Léman wants one number from you.

You've just joined Café Léman — twelve cafés around Lake Geneva, about 21,000 loyalty members, and a sustainability promise it takes seriously — as Head of Operations. In your first board meeting, the numbers fly:

  • Single-use cups are the largest single item in the company's waste footprint.
  • The proposal on the table: a CHF 2 deposit, reusable-cup programme across all twelve cafés.

Why this course exists

Notice what just happened in that meeting: the loudest claim — "everyone I talk to would join" — was built on a tiny, biased, unmeasured sample. That's not a character flaw; it's the default way humans reason.

Quantitative methods is the discipline of doing better: collecting data deliberately, describing it honestly, and only then generalising — with the uncertainty stated, not hidden. Every tool in the next ten weeks serves one purpose: decisions you can defend — to a board, a bank, or a regulator.

A number you can defend beats a number you believe.

Section 1.2 · Overview of Statistics

The two jobs of statistics

The core distinction

Describing what you saw vs. claiming what you didn't.

Everything in this course is one of two moves:

  • Descriptive statistics summarises data you actually have. "Average spend across our 12 cafés last month was CHF 11.40." No leap, no risk — arithmetic on facts.
  • Inferential statistics uses a sample to make claims about a population you did not fully observe. "We estimate 30–38% of all loyalty members would join." That's a leap — and the whole machinery of the course exists to make that leap honest.

Two more words you'll use every week: a number that describes the population is a parameter (usually unknowable); the same number computed from your sample is a statistic (your best evidence about it). Confusing the two is the most common error in business presentations.

The process

From question to decision — five steps you'll repeat all term.

  • 1 · Frame the business question. "Should we launch the cup programme?" becomes "What share of members would join, and at what cost per return?" Vague questions produce vague data.
  • 2 · Define the population and the variables. Who exactly counts as "our customers"? What exactly will you measure, in what units? (Section 1.3.)
  • 3 · Collect the data — deliberately, knowing every method's blind spots. (Section 1.4.)
  • 4 · Describe it — tables, pictures (Section 1.5), then numbers (Week 2).
  • 5 · Infer and decide — generalise with stated uncertainty (Weeks 3–9), then commit.

Most real-world failures happen in steps 1–3, before any formula is touched. That's why this whole first week lives there.

Statistics done well is mostly careful thinking done early.

Section 1.3 · Types of Data

Know your variables

Why it matters

The type of a variable decides what maths you're allowed to do.

Café Léman's loyalty database has a column for home café. You could code the cafés 1–12 and compute "average café = 6.4". The software won't stop you. The result is meaningless — and dashboards full of confident nonsense start exactly here.

Every variable is either categorical (labels: home café, cup size) or numerical (counts and measurements: visits, spend). Numerical variables are discrete (countable — visits per week) or continuous (any value in a range — spend in CHF). And each sits on one of four levels of measurement:

  • Nominal — names only. You can count and compare frequencies. Home café.
  • Ordinal — ordered labels, but gaps between them aren't equal. Medians and rankings, yes; means, be careful. Cup size S/M/L; satisfaction 1–5.
  • Interval — equal gaps, but zero is arbitrary, so ratios are meaningless. 20°C is not "twice as hot" as 10°C. Temperature.
  • Ratio — equal gaps and a true zero, so everything works, including "twice as much". Spend, visits, revenue.
Two more distinctions you'll use weekly

Discrete vs continuous · snapshot vs film

Discrete numerical variables are counts — visits per week, cups returned. They jump in whole steps; you can't visit 2.4 times. Continuous variables are measurements — spend in CHF, minutes in the queue — and can land anywhere in a range. The distinction matters from Week 3 on: discrete and continuous variables get entirely different probability models.

Separately: cross-sectional data is a snapshot — June revenue for each of the 12 cafés (many units, one moment). Time-series data is a film — the Vevey café's revenue every month since 2020 (one unit, many moments). They answer different questions ("who is ahead?" vs "where is this going?"), they're charted differently (bars vs lines), and Week 8 is devoted entirely to the second kind.

The satisfaction-score trap

You classified satisfaction 1–5 as ordinal — correct. Yet every company on earth reports "average satisfaction 4.2". Is everyone wrong? Strictly, yes; pragmatically, it's a modelling choice: treating the five steps as equally spaced. Often harmless, sometimes not — the honest move is to know you're making that assumption and check the full distribution too, not just the mean. This is the first of many places where statistics is a choice you're responsible for, not a lookup table.

Section 1.4 · Data Collection

Where numbers are born

Garbage in, confident garbage out

No analysis can rescue badly collected data.

Before trusting any dataset, interrogate its birth. Was it collected for this question (primary data — your survey, your pilot) or borrowed from elsewhere (secondary data — industry reports, the POS system)? Did you observe everyone (census) or a subset (sample)? And if a sample — who got picked, and who picked themselves?

The gold standard is the simple random sample: every member of the population has an equal chance of selection. Practical variants — systematic (every k-th customer), stratified (sample within each café, then combine), cluster (pick whole cafés at random) — trade a little purity for feasibility. The dangerous one is the convenience sample: whoever was easy to reach. It's fast, it's cheap, and it quietly answers a different question than the one you asked.

From population to sample, mechanically

The sampling frame — and how a random sample actually gets drawn

You can't sample from "our customers" as an idea; you need a sampling frame — a concrete list to draw from. For Café Léman that's the loyalty database (21,000 rows). Note the quiet gap already opening: loyalty members ≠ all customers. Every frame misses someone, and whoever it misses is invisible to your survey no matter how well you sample.

From the frame, the practical designs:

  • Simple random sample — number the list 1–21,000, let a random generator pick 400. The gold standard.
  • Systematic sample — random start, then every k-th member. With k = 21,000 ÷ 400 ≈ every 52nd row, you get 400 names with almost no effort — nearly as good as random, provided the list has no hidden pattern.
  • Stratified sample — sample ~33 members within each of the 12 cafés, then combine. Guarantees every café is heard from; small cafés can't vanish by chance.
  • Cluster sample — pick 3 whole cafés at random and survey everyone there. Cheap for face-to-face work, but cafés differ, so you're betting the 3 you drew are typical.
  • Convenience sample — whoever was easy. Not a design; a hope.

Three ways surveys poison themselves
  • Selection bias — the people you can reach differ from the people you care about. Morning regulars are your most loyal customers; of course they say yes.
  • Nonresponse / self-selection bias — the people who bother to answer differ from those who don't. An Instagram poll is answered by fans, by definition.
  • Wording & measurement bias — "Would you help us fight plastic waste by joining?" is not a question, it's a nudge. So are leading scales, double-barrelled questions, and asking in front of the barista.

None of these show up in the spreadsheet afterwards. The data looks identical — clean columns, plausible numbers. The flaw lives in the collection, so the check must happen there too.

Section 1.5 · Describing Data Visually

The first honest look

Chart grammar

The question chooses the chart.

  • How do categories compare? → bar chart. (Pie charts only for a handful of parts-of-a-whole — and never in 3-D.)
  • How is a numerical variable distributed? → histogram. Always your first look at any measurement.
  • How did it change over time? → line chart.
  • How do two numerical variables relate? → scatter plot.

The histogram deserves special respect: it's how you discover that "average daily cups: 245" is hiding two completely different businesses. See for yourself.

Before the picture: the table

Frequency tables — how a histogram is built

A histogram is just a frequency table drawn as bars. You choose classes (equal-width intervals covering the data), count how many observations fall in each (frequency), and divide by the total for the share (relative frequency). Here are the 60 days of pilot-café cup sales from the lab below, in classes of width 50:

Read the highlighted row: not a single day landed between 250 and 299 — yet the overall average is about 245. The empty middle is the loudest row in the table, and a mean would never have told you. Rules of thumb: 5–12 classes, equal widths, no gaps, no overlaps — and always show the total.

Try it

Choose a bin width

With narrow bins the truth appears: a weekday café (around 220 cups) and a weekend café (around 330 cups) living in the same building. Any plan built on "an average day of 245 cups" — staffing, stock, cup inventory — would be wrong every single day. Note also: bin width was your choice, and it changed what you saw. Even honest pictures involve judgement.

The axis trick

Same data, two stories

Marketing wants to show the board how the share of plant-based milk has grown over six months. Toggle between the two versions they could send.

Where does the axis start?

The growth is real — about 3 points in six months — but the left version shouts surge while the right whispers drift. The rule: bar charts must start at zero, because bars encode value by length. Line charts may zoom in to show detail — but only with the axis clearly labelled. When you read charts, your first glance goes to the axes; when you make them, so does your integrity.

Charts don't lie. Axes do.

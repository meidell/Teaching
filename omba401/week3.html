Section 3.1 · The Hook

The "95% accurate" trap

Your decision

Should you auto-ban a flagged loyalty card?

Café Léman's app has a fraud filter for stolen loyalty cards. IT reports it is 95% accurate: it flags 95% of genuinely stolen cards, and wrongly flags only 5% of legitimate ones. A card just got flagged. The CEO wants to auto-ban every flagged account on the spot.

You pause, because one number is missing from the room: stolen cards are rare — only about 1% of cards are actually stolen. Before we compute anything: if a card is flagged, what's the probability it is really stolen?

Commit — then lock it
Pick your gut answer.
Locked. The instinct is "95%" — and it's badly wrong. Because stolen cards are so rare, most flags are false alarms. Picture 10,000 cards: ~100 are stolen (95 get flagged), and ~9,900 are legitimate (but 5% = ~495 get flagged too). So of ~590 flags, only ~95 are real — about 16%. Auto-banning would punish five legitimate customers for every real thief. You'll build exactly this reasoning by hand in §3.3 — no formula required. This week is about never being fooled by a probability again.
From "what happened" to "what might"

Descriptive → probabilistic.

Weeks 1–2 were descriptive: you summarised data you already held. Probability is the bridge to inference (from Week 4), because it is the mathematics of uncertainty. This week builds it in four plain-English moves:

  • The rules — how chances combine (§3.2).
  • Updating — how a new fact changes a chance: Bayes, done by counting (§3.3).
  • Counting outcomes — the shapes behind "how many": binomial & Poisson (§3.4).
  • The bell curve — the normal distribution behind almost everything to come (§3.5).

A probability is a disciplined opinion about the future. This week is how to make it honest.

Section 3.2 · The Rules

How chances combine

Three everyday moves

You already do this in your head — here it is, named.

Almost every probability question is one of three simple moves. No symbols needed to grasp them:

  • The flip side (complement). "What's the chance it doesn't happen?" is just 1 minus the chance it does. If 30% of members redeem a coupon, 70% don't. This is the shortcut for any "at least one" question.
  • This or that (addition). To get the chance of A or B, add them — but if they can happen together, subtract the overlap once, so you don't count it twice.
  • This and then that (multiplication). To get the chance of A and B both happening, multiply — using the chance of B given A already happened.
The classic confusion

Mutually exclusive is not the same as independent.

Students mix these two up constantly — and they're almost opposites. The cleanest way to feel the difference:

  • Mutually exclusive = can't happen together. A single payment is card or cash, never both. Learn it was card, and the chance it was cash drops to zero — so one does affect the other.
  • Independent = don't affect each other. One customer paying by card tells you nothing about whether the next one will. Learn the first, and the second's chance is unchanged.

So "one event tells you nothing about the other" (independent) is the opposite of "one rules the other out" (mutually exclusive). The quick test: they're independent when knowing B leaves A's chance exactly as it was.

Worked example — read a two-way table

Every probability you need is already in the grid.

You never need to memorise which rule to apply if you have the counts. Here are 1,000 loyalty members, split by weekend-vs-weekday visiting and whether they redeemed a coupon:

WeekendWeekdayTotal
Redeemed180120300

Read probabilities straight off the grid — just pick your denominator:

  • P(redeemed) = 300 out of 1000 = 0.30 — a whole row over the grand total.
  • P(redeemed and weekend) = 180 out of 1000 = 0.18 — a single cell.
  • P(redeemed or weekend) = 0.30 + 0.40 − 0.18 = 0.52 — add the row and column, subtract the shared cell.
  • P(redeemed given weekend) = 180 out of 400 = 0.45 — now the denominator is just the weekend column.

That last move is the big one: "given weekend" throws away everyone else and only looks inside the weekend column. And notice — weekend visitors redeem at 45% versus 30% overall, so the two are not independent. Weekend tells you something.

Section 3.3 · Updating Beliefs

The flagged card, by counting

Don't compute — count

Turn the percentages into people.

Here's the trick the pros use, and it needs no formula: whenever a "test" gives you a result and you want to know what it really means, stop thinking in percentages and imagine a crowd of real cases.

So picture 10,000 Café Léman cards. We were told three things: about 1% are actually stolen, the filter flags 95% of stolen cards, and it wrongly flags 5% of legit ones. Walk it through, in people:

  • 100 cards are stolen → the filter flags 95 of them.
  • 9,900 cards are fine → but 5% get flagged anyway, that's 495 false alarms.
  • So 590 cards get flagged in total — and only 95 of them were really stolen.

A flagged card is truly stolen just 95 out of 590 ≈ 16% of the time. The "95% accurate" filter produces five false alarms for every real catch — not because it's a bad filter, but because it's hunting something rare. That's the whole idea; the lab below lets you feel it move.

Same filter, same accuracy — but the answer swings from near-zero to near-certain purely on how common the thing is. That's why the base rate is never optional.

So what do you tell the CEO?

Auto-banning every flagged account would wrongly hit about five loyal customers for every real thief. The number just made the decision for you: don't ban on a flag — trigger a gentle "please verify your card" step instead, and reserve bans for cards that fail that too.

This is the entire job of a manager who understands probability: the filter didn't change, but knowing what a flag really means changed the policy.

If you like symbols. What you just did by counting is exactly Bayes' theorem: P(stolen | flagged) = 95 ÷ 590 ≈ 0.16. Written formally, P(A|B) = P(B|A)·P(A) / P(B) — but the crowd of 10,000 gives the same answer and you'll never misremember it.

Section 3.4 · Counting Outcomes

Discrete distributions

The shape of "how many"

You send 20 coupons. How many come back?

Each of 20 members has, say, a 30% chance of redeeming. On average that's 6 — but you'd never bet it's exactly 6 every time. Some weeks 4 come back, some weeks 9. A distribution is simply the picture of that whole range: every possible count, and how likely each one is.

Two everyday shapes

"Out of a fixed number" vs "over a stretch of time."

The shape you just built has a name, and it has a cousin. The difference is just the question you're asking:

  • Binomial — successes out of a fixed number of tries. How many of 20 coupons redeem? How many of 12 cups in a box are faulty? You have a set number of "attempts", each a yes/no with the same chance.
  • Poisson — events over a stretch of time or space. How many customers arrive in the next 10 minutes? How many complaints this week? There's no fixed number of tries — just an average rate, and you count what turns up.

That's the whole choice: a fixed set of yes/no trials (binomial), or a count of things happening at some rate (Poisson).

Worked example — what's it worth?

The expected value of a promo.

A promo wheel pays a customer CHF 0 most of the time (70%), CHF 10 sometimes (20%), and CHF 50 rarely (10%). What does it cost you per play, on average? Weight each prize by its chance and add:

(0 × 0.70) + (10 × 0.20) + (50 × 0.10) = 0 + 2 + 5 = CHF 7

That single number — the expected value — is all you need to budget the campaign or price the offer. No individual player wins exactly CHF 7; it's the long-run average across thousands of plays.

Section 3.5 · The Bell Curve

The normal distribution

A universal ruler

How do you compare a busy day to a big spender?

A café sells 239 cups on a Saturday. A member spends CHF 120 in a month. Which is more unusual? You can't compare cups to francs directly — different units. So statisticians use one shared ruler: how many standard deviations from average is it? That number is the z-score.

A z-score of +1 means "one step of the usual wobble above average"; +2 means "two steps out — genuinely high"; a negative z means below average. Cups, francs, heights, exam marks — once you speak in z-scores, everything is on the same scale, and "+2" always means roughly the same kind of unusual.

The one-line recipe: z = (value − average) ÷ standard deviation. A 239-cup day, when the café averages 193 with a wobble of 23, is (239 − 193) ÷ 23 = +2.0 — two steps above normal.
Where the ruler comes from

The bell curve, and the 68–95–99.7 habit.

Lots of business quantities — daily sales, delivery times, measurement errors — pile up in a symmetric bell shape: most values near the average, fewer as you go out, a mirror image on each side. For any bell, one rule of thumb does enormous work (you met it in Week 2 as the empirical rule):

  • about 68% of values land within 1 step (SD) of the average;
  • about 95% within 2 steps;
  • about 99.7% within 3 steps.

So a day beyond 2 steps out is already in the rarest ~5% — which is why a z-score of +2 or more makes you look twice. For a continuous quantity like this, a probability is an area under the curve: slide a cut-off and the shaded slice is the chance.

Your Excel toolkit

When you need the exact number, not just the picture.

The intuition lives in the labs above; these are the functions for a precise answer. TRUE gives a cumulative chance (≤), FALSE gives an exact one (=).

TaskExcel
Combinations C(n,k)=COMBIN(n,k)
Binomial P(X = k)=BINOM.DIST(k,n,p,FALSE)
Binomial P(X ≤ k)=BINOM.DIST(k,n,p,TRUE)
Poisson P(X = k)=POISSON.DIST(k,λ,FALSE)
Normal P(X ≤ x)=NORM.DIST(x,μ,σ,TRUE)
Standard normal from a z=NORM.S.DIST(z,TRUE)
Value at a percentile=NORM.INV(p,μ,σ)
z-score of a value=(x−μ)/σ

Tip: for "chance above x" use 1 − NORM.DIST(x,μ,σ,TRUE); for "between a and b", subtract the two cumulatives.

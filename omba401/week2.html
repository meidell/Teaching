Descriptive statistics: centre, spread & position

The loyalty data has landed on your desk at Café Léman, and the CEO wants "one number" to describe the customer. This week you learn why one number is never enough — and how to turn a cloud of numbers into a summary you can defend: the right measure of centre, an honest measure of spread, and the tools of position that flag what's unusual. Plan 4–5 hours: ~two for this lesson, its four videos, worked examples and labs, then 2–3 for the homework below. Forum Activity 1 opens on Moodle this week.

By the end of Week 2 you will be able to

  • Choose between the mean, median and mode — and know why a few "whales" make the mean lie.
  • Compute a weighted mean, and avoid the "average of averages" trap.
  • Measure spread with the range, IQR, variance and standard deviation — by hand — and know when to use the sample vs population formula.
  • Locate any value with position tools: quartiles, percentiles and the z-score, and compare across different scales.
  • Apply the empirical rule and Chebyshev's theorem, and read (and draw) a five-number summary / boxplot.
  • Spot skew from the mean-vs-median gap, flag outliers with the 1.5×IQR rule, and decide what to do with them.
  • Reproduce every measure in Excel.

Section 2.1 · The Hook

One number for the board

Your decision

"What does a typical customer spend?"

The loyalty data is finally in. At the board meeting, the CEO wants a single figure for "typical monthly spend per member". You pull two numbers from the same column:

  • The mean (average) monthly spend: CHF 95.
  • The median monthly spend: CHF 62.

Both are correct. Both describe the same customers. They disagree by more than 50%, because a handful of corporate accounts and superfans — "whales" — spend enormously and drag the average up. Which one do you put on the slide?

Commit — then lock it
Pick what you'd tell the board.
Locked. The honest answer is usually both — but if forced to pick one "typical" figure, the median wins: it's the spend of the middle customer, untouched by the whales. The mean isn't wrong, it just answers a different question (total ÷ heads), and quietly overstates what a normal member spends. Reporting only the mean is how a "healthy average" hides a business where most customers spend far less. This week is about never being fooled by a single number again.
The four questions every summary answers

Centre, spread, position — and shape.

Week 1 gave you the first picture of the data. This week gives you the numbers that summarise it, and the judgement to pick the right ones. A defensible summary answers four questions:

  • Where does it sit? — the centre (§2.2).
  • How scattered is it? — the spread (§2.3).
  • Where does one value rank? — its position (§2.4).
  • What's the overall form? — the shape, and any outliers (§2.5).

Miss any one and your summary can mislead — as the mean just did. Everything this week is still descriptive (summarising data you hold); from Week 3 you'll turn these numbers into inferential claims about customers you haven't measured.

A single statistic is an opinion about your data. Descriptive statistics is choosing the honest one.

Section 2.2 · Centre

Mean, median & mode

Three kinds of "average"

They agree when data is symmetric — and part ways when it isn't.

  • Mean — add every value, divide by the count. Uses all the data, but every extreme value pulls it.
  • Median — the middle value once sorted. Ignores how extreme the extremes are, so it's robust to outliers.
  • Mode — the most frequent value. The only centre that works for categorical data (most common cup size), and the way to spot a bimodal dataset (two peaks — like the weekday/weekend café from Week 1).
Mean x̄ = (Σx) ÷ n  ·  Median = middle of the sorted values  ·  Mode = most frequent

The gap between the mean and median is itself information: it's your first read on skew (section 2.5).

Worked example

Mean & median of one week, by hand.

Seven days of spend at one till (CHF): 58, 61, 64, 60, 300, 59, 62. The Wednesday 300 is a corporate catering order.

  1. Mean: sum = 58+61+64+60+300+59+62 = 664; ÷ 7 = CHF 94.86.
  2. Median: sort → 58, 59, 60, 61, 62, 64, 300. The 4th of 7 is the middle → CHF 61.

One catering order pushes the mean to CHF 95 while the median stays at a realistic CHF 61 — the board slide in miniature. Notice the mean isn't even close to any actual day.

The weighted mean

Not all averages should count equally.

The CEO asks for the chain-wide average spend. You have each café's average and its number of visits:

CaféAvg spend (CHF)Visits
Genève601,000
Nyon80500
Vevey502,500

The tempting "average of the averages" is (60 + 80 + 50) ÷ 3 = CHF 63.3 — and it's wrong, because it treats tiny Nyon and huge Vevey as equals. The weighted mean weights each café by its visits:

x̄w = Σ(wᵢ·xᵢ) ÷ Σwᵢ = (60·1000 + 80·500 + 50·2500) ÷ 4000 = 225,000 ÷ 4000 = CHF 56.25

The real chain-wide figure is CHF 56.25, not 63.3 — because most visits happen at low-spend Vevey. Whenever you average numbers that already summarise different-sized groups, weight them.

Section 2.3 · Spread

Range, IQR & standard deviation

Centre is only half the story

Two cafés can share an average and run completely differently.

  • Range = max − min. Instant, but hostage to a single extreme value.
  • Interquartile range (IQR) = Q3 − Q1: the span of the middle 50%. Robust, like the median.
  • Variance and standard deviation — the average distance of values from the mean. The standard deviation (SD) is in the original units, so it's the one you quote.
  • Coefficient of variation = SD ÷ mean: spread as a % of the average, so you can compare a café's spend variability with its footfall variability.
s = √[ Σ(x − x̄)² ÷ (n − 1) ]  ·  IQR = Q3 − Q1  ·  CV = (s ÷ x̄) × 100%

Why square the deviations?

To measure spread you want the "typical distance from the mean". But the deviations (x − x̄) always sum to zero — the positives and negatives cancel. Squaring fixes that: squares are never negative, so they can't cancel, and squaring also punishes big misses more than small ones. The average squared deviation is the variance; taking the square root brings us back to the original units — the standard deviation.

Worked example

Standard deviation, step by step.

The "consistent café" from the lab: five days of spend 57, 59, 61, 63, 65. Mean = 305 ÷ 5 = 61. Build the deviation table:

Sample variance s² = Σ(x−x̄)² ÷ (n−1) = 40 ÷ 4 = 10. Standard deviation s = √10 = 3.16 CHF. (Note the middle column sums to zero — the reason we squared.)

Sample vs population — why n − 1?

You'll see two versions of the formula, and picking the wrong one is a classic slip:

  • Sample standard deviation s divides by n − 1. Use it when your data is a sample standing in for a bigger population — almost always, in business. (Excel: STDEV.S.)
  • Population standard deviation σ divides by n. Use it only when your data is the whole population. (Excel: STDEV.P.)

Dividing by n − 1 (the "Bessel correction") nudges the estimate up slightly, correcting for the fact that a sample hugs its own mean a little too tightly. With small n the difference is real: for the five values above, dividing by 5 instead of 4 gives σ = √8 = 2.83 instead of s = 3.16. When in doubt, you have a sample — use n − 1.

Section 2.4 · Position

Quartiles, z-scores & boxplots

Locating a single value

Centre and spread describe the crowd; position pins one person in it.

  • Percentiles & quartiles — the p-th percentile has p% of values below it. Q1, Q2 (median) and Q3 cut the data into quarters.
  • Five-number summary — min, Q1, median, Q3, max: the skeleton of a boxplot.
  • z-score = (x − mean) ÷ SD: how many standard deviations a value sits from the mean. It makes different scales comparable — a spend and a visit-count on one ruler.
  • The empirical rule — for bell-shaped data, ≈68% of values fall within 1 SD of the mean, ≈95% within 2, ≈99.7% within 3.
z = (x − x̄) ÷ s  ·  five-number summary: min · Q1 · median · Q3 · max

Worked example

Comparing apples to oranges with z-scores.

Two members are both "your best". Which is more exceptional?

  • Ava spends CHF 120/month, where members average CHF 60 (SD 12). z = (120 − 60) ÷ 12 = +5.0.
  • Ben visits 30×/month, where members average 6 visits (SD 4). z = (30 − 6) ÷ 4 = +6.0.

Different units, one ruler: Ben (z = 6.0) is more exceptional than Ava (z = 5.0), even though the raw numbers look nothing alike. That's the whole power of standardising — the z-score is the currency for comparing across scales, and it's the bridge to the normal distribution in Week 3.

When the data isn't bell-shaped: Chebyshev

A rule that holds for any shape.

The empirical rule needs an approximately bell-shaped distribution. Café spend is right-skewed, so it doesn't strictly apply. Chebyshev's theorem is the safety net — it holds for any distribution:

At least (1 − 1/k²) of all values lie within k standard deviations of the mean (for k > 1).
  • k = 2 → at least 1 − 1/4 = 75% of values within 2 SD.
  • k = 3 → at least 1 − 1/9 ≈ 89% within 3 SD.

Chebyshev gives a weaker guarantee (75% vs the empirical rule's 95% at k = 2) precisely because it asks nothing about shape. Use the empirical rule when the histogram looks bell-shaped; fall back to Chebyshev when it doesn't.

Section 2.5 · Shape & Outliers

Read the shape, catch the outliers

Skew: what the mean-vs-median gap tells you

The two averages point toward the tail.

  • Right-skewed (tail to the right): a few large values pull the mean above the median. Café Léman spend, income, house prices.
  • Roughly symmetric: mean ≈ median. Heights, many measurement errors.
  • Left-skewed (tail to the left): a few small values pull the mean below the median. Exam scores where most do well.

So you can often diagnose shape without a chart at all — just compare the mean and the median. (Then check the picture anyway; a symmetric mean and median can still hide two peaks, as the Week 1 café did.)

The outlier rule you can defend

1.5 × IQR — a boundary, not a delete key.

The standard flag: a value is a candidate outlier if it falls below Q1 − 1.5 × IQR or above Q3 + 1.5 × IQR. These are the "fences" a boxplot's whiskers stop at.

lower fence = Q1 − 1.5·IQR  ·  upper fence = Q3 + 1.5·IQR

Describe first, judge second, delete almost never.

Worked example

Hunt for outliers in Dataset A.

From §2.4: Q1 = 176.5, Q3 = 211, so IQR = 34.5.

  1. 1.5 × IQR = 1.5 × 34.5 = 51.75.
  2. Lower fence = 176.5 − 51.75 = 124.75.
  3. Upper fence = 211 + 51.75 = 262.75.
  4. Check the extremes: min = 152 (> 124.75 ✓) and max = 236 (< 262.75 ✓).

Every day sits inside the fences, so Dataset A has no outliers — which matches the boxplot in §2.4, whose whiskers stopped short of the gold fences. You'll redo this yourself in the homework.

Three kinds of outlier — and what to do

Finding an outlier is the start of the work, not the end. There are three very different kinds:

  • An error. A member "spends" CHF 999,999 — a typo or a system glitch. Correct it or drop it, and note that you did.
  • Genuine but unrepresentative. A one-off CHF 5,000 corporate catering order. Keep it, but report it separately so it doesn't distort the "typical customer" story.
  • The most important point you have. The whale who alone is 8% of revenue. Never delete this — it may be the single most valuable thing in the dataset. Investigate it.

Silently dropping outliers to make a chart tidy is one of the quiet ways numbers lie — the mirror image of the axis tricks from Week 1. The honest move is always to say what you did and why.

Your Excel toolkit

Every measure this week, as a formula.

If your data sits in cells A2:A21, these are the functions to reach for. Business analysts almost always use the sample versions.

MeasureExcel
Mean=AVERAGE(A2:A21)
Weighted mean=SUMPRODUCT(x,w)/SUM(w)
Median=MEDIAN(A2:A21)
Mode=MODE.SNGL(A2:A21)
Range=MAX(A2:A21)−MIN(A2:A21)
Q1 / Q3=QUARTILE.INC(A2:A21,1) / …,3)
IQR=QUARTILE.INC(…,3)−QUARTILE.INC(…,1)
Variance (sample)=VAR.S(A2:A21)
Std dev (sample / pop.)=STDEV.S(…) / =STDEV.P(…)
Percentile=PERCENTILE.INC(A2:A21,0.9)
z-score of a value=(x−AVERAGE(…))/STDEV.S(…)

Tip: Data → Data Analysis → Descriptive Statistics outputs most of these at once — but knowing the formulas is how you check it's right.

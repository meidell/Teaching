AI & project management: how project managers actually adopt ChatGPT

A 2024 survey of 131 Italian project managers asks a very practical question: when generative AI lands on a PM's desk, what makes some embrace it creatively and others ignore it? The answer is three human factors — and none of them is the technology itself.

At a glance

Authors Felicetti, Cimino, Mazzoleni & Ammirato (Universities of Calabria, Salento & Brescia)
Published 2024 · Journal of Innovation & Knowledge (open access)
Method Survey of 131 project managers (PMI Southern Italy Chapter) · analysed with PLS-SEM
Read ~10 pages. Focus on the research model (Fig. 1), the six hypotheses, and the practical implications.

Why it's on this course. Session 1 argued AI projects fail for managerial, not technical, reasons. This paper is the evidence in miniature: whether a generative-AI tool creates value depends on the PM's mindset, their peers, and how well the tool fits the task — exactly the human variables you'll manage all course.

The four things to remember
1
Adoption is a human process, not an install. Using the Adaptive Structuration Theory lens, the study shows the same tool (ChatGPT) produces very different results depending on how each PM appropriates it.
2
Three factors drive appropriation: a PM's Innovation Attitude, their Peer Influence, and Task-Technology Fit. All three were confirmed to matter.
3
Task-Technology Fit matters most. When the tool genuinely fits the job, PMs use it both creatively and intensively — fit had the strongest effect of the three; peer influence the weakest.
4
"Unfaithful" use is a good thing. Bending a tool beyond its intended use ("unfaithful appropriation") is not misuse — it's creative reinvention, and it's where much of AI's project value comes from.
The research model in one picture
Three drivers → two kinds of adoption
Innovation Attitude (IA)
the PM's openness to new tech
Peer Influence (PI)
colleagues modelling AI use
Task-Technology Fit (TTF)
how well the tool suits the task
→
Creative Behaviour (CB)
novel, inventive uses in projects
Unfaithful Appropriation (UFOA)
committed use beyond the tool's original intent

All six hypothesised links were statistically supported. The strength order: TTF > IA > PI. In plain terms — fit the tool to the job first, cultivate an experimental mindset second, and use peer example third.

The three drivers, explained
Driver 1

Innovation Attitude — the experimental mindset

PMs who are naturally curious and open to new tools are more likely both to use AI creatively and to push it beyond its intended use. Implication: organisations can grow this — training that builds confidence and an experimental disposition pays off directly in AI value.

Driver 2

Peer Influence — seeing colleagues succeed

When PMs watch peers apply AI tools cleverly, it sets a norm and inspires them to try. The effect is real but the weakest of the three — social proof helps, but it can't substitute for fit or mindset. Implication: share success stories and run peer-led knowledge exchanges.

Driver 3 · strongest

Task-Technology Fit — does the tool suit the job?

When a PM perceives that the AI genuinely fits their tasks, engagement deepens — leading to both creative and committed use. This was the strongest driver. Implication: don't deploy AI generically; match specific tools to specific PM tasks (planning, risk analysis, stakeholder comms) and make that fit visible.

Fit first. A brilliant tool used on the wrong task creates nothing; an ordinary tool matched to a real need transforms the work.

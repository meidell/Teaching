AI adoption in SMEs vs large firms — why size changes everything

A 2025 review of 78 studies asks whether small and large firms adopt AI the same way. They don't. Same technology, very different projects — and knowing which world your project lives in tells you which barriers you'll actually hit.

At a glance

Published 2025 · Journal of Innovation & Knowledge (open access)
Method Systematic review + bibliometric analysis of 78 peer-reviewed articles (Scopus & Web of Science), via the TOE framework

Why it's on this course. The same AI project is a different beast in a 30-person firm than in a multinational. This review gives you a checklist — the TOE framework and its 10 dimensions — for diagnosing what will actually make or break AI adoption in a given organisation. It's a planning tool, not just a literature summary.

The four things to remember
1
Organisational size shapes AI adoption more than almost anything else. Resources, data, skills and governance all scale with size — so the same project faces different obstacles in an SME versus a large firm.
2
The TOE framework organises the whole picture: every adoption factor is Technological, Organisational, or Environmental. The review sorts the evidence into 10 dimensions across these three.
3
Large firms optimise; SMEs solve immediate problems. Large firms use AI for big-data and large-scale operations; SMEs prioritise practical wins — chatbots, inventory, automating routine tasks — and need plug-and-play ease of use.
4
SMEs' biggest barriers are data, skills and money. They often lack the data volume, in-house talent and capital for sophisticated AI — so they lean on partnerships and outside consultants. Large firms' hidden enemy is inertia.
The framework · TOE and its 10 dimensions

Every factor influencing AI adoption falls under one of three headings — the Technology–Organisation–Environment model:

Technology technology readiness · customization · AI tools & needs · data requirements
Organisation skills & competencies · financial readiness · management support
Environment market & competitive pressure · partnerships & collaboration · regulatory compliance

Ten dimensions in total. Walking a project through them is a fast way to find where adoption will really struggle.

The core comparison · same AI, two worlds

Large firms

  • Advanced infrastructure & capital for sophisticated AI
  • Huge datasets → predictive analytics at scale
  • Build in-house AI teams
  • Use AI for big-data ops, logistics, optimisation
  • Better placed on data-privacy & compliance
  • But: organisational inertia & slow decisions

SMEs

  • Limited infrastructure, capital & data
  • Need user-friendly, plug-and-play tools
  • Rely on vendors, consultants & partnerships
  • Use AI for chatbots, inventory, routine automation
  • Often lack compliance mechanisms → need guidance
  • But: agile, fewer layers, faster to act

Before you plan an AI project, ask which world it lives in. The technology may be identical; the barriers never are.

The numbers & the takeaway

The review notes AI adopters report roughly 30% higher annual revenue growth than non-adopters — the prize that keeps organisations of every size investing. But the author's central conclusion is that one-size-fits-all AI strategies fail: SMEs and large firms need tailored approaches and support matched to their distinct constraints. For policymakers, that means targeted help for SMEs on data, skills and financing.

Section 2.1 · The hook

"Just give me a Gantt chart"

Your decision

The COO wants a date you can't honestly promise

Session 1 got the fraud project framed. Now MekongPay's COO says: "Great — give me a Gantt chart and a go-live date by Friday." The trouble is that the biggest, least predictable part of your project hasn't even started, and it isn't the modelling.

Before you answer, an instinct check: across a typical AI project, what share of the total effort goes into data work — acquiring, cleaning and preparing the data?

Your estimate
40%
Locked. Practitioners routinely put data preparation at 60–80% of total project effort — before a single production model exists. That's why a plan that treats "build the model" as the big task is already wrong: the schedule, the budget and the risk all live in the data. Planning an AI project means planning the experiments and the data work honestly — not drawing a tidy waterfall and hoping.
Why this session exists

A plan you can defend beats a date you can only promise. This session gives you the four tools to build that defensible plan: a lifecycle to structure the work, clear requirements to scope it, a methodology to match its uncertainty, and a schedule that builds the uncertainty in rather than denying it.

You cannot schedule what you pretend is certain. Plan the experiments, buffer the unknowns, and tell the truth about both.

Section 2.2 · The lifecycle

Frameworks that structure the work

The eight stages

Every AI project moves through the same logical stages

Session 1 met the lifecycle at a glance; here is the fuller version project managers actually plan against:

1 · Problem
define the objective & success criteria
2 · Data acquisition
availability, ownership, compliance
3 · Data prep
clean, engineer features (60–80% of effort)
4 · Model dev
select, train, tune
5 · Validation
precision/recall vs business goals
6 · Deployment
integrate into operations
7 · Monitoring
watch for drift
8 · Retraining
update & redeploy

The project manager coordinates all eight — not just the modelling. The stages loop: validation sends you back to data, monitoring back to retraining.

Three frameworks, same spine

CRISP-DM, TDSP and MLOps

  • CRISP-DM — the classic six-phase data-mining cycle (Business Understanding → Data Understanding → Data Preparation → Modeling → Evaluation → Deployment). Iterative, business-anchored, industry-agnostic.
  • TDSP (Microsoft's Team Data Science Process) — adds team collaboration, standardised documentation and customer acceptance for enterprise scale.
  • MLOps — extends the lifecycle past deployment into continuous monitoring and automated retraining, connecting machine learning with DevOps.

Frameworks differ in vocabulary, not logic. Pick one as your shared language — the point is that everyone plans against the same stages.

Section 2.3 · Scope

Scope & requirements

From business problem to spec

Clear scope is what keeps AI and the business pointed the same way

Vague scope is the number-one way AI projects drift, overrun and disappoint. Good scoping translates a business goal into three kinds of requirement, plus a structure to build against:

  • Functional requirements — what the system does: "classify each transaction as fraud / not-fraud."
  • Non-functional requirements — how well it must perform: "score in under 200 ms; handle 10k concurrent users; be explainable to the regulator."
  • Data requirements — what data, how much, how labelled: "3 years of transactions, with confirmed-fraud labels."

A Work Breakdown Structure (WBS) then splits the project into manageable deliverables — problem definition, data acquisition & prep, model dev & validation, deployment & monitoring — clarifying dependencies and who owns what.

The trap

Requirements that evolve — and scope creep

AI projects are experimental, so requirements legitimately evolve as the data reveals what's possible. That's healthy — but only with a change-control process: document the change, review its impact on time/cost/quality, approve it explicitly. Adding "just one more data source" or "also predict churn while you're at it" with no trade-off is scope creep, and it sinks schedules.

Let the requirements evolve on purpose. What you cannot allow is scope changing by accident.

Section 2.4 · Scheduling

Scheduling under uncertainty

Why AI scheduling is different

Timelines are probabilistic, not deterministic

Nobody can promise how many training cycles it takes to hit acceptable accuracy, or whether the data will cooperate. A traditional fixed timeline breaks on first contact. AI scheduling instead uses:

  • Iterative milestones — prototype, validation results, pilot deployment — adaptive checkpoints, not a fixed march.
  • Critical Path Method — find the tasks that directly gate go-live, and watch the risky ones (model training/testing) closely.
  • Gantt charts with buffers — visualise tasks and dependencies, and deliberately add buffer time at the risky phases.
  • Parallel vs sequential — run what you can at once (data prep, infra setup, early experiments); respect true dependencies (you can't deploy before you validate).

Buffers aren't padding you apologise for — they're the honest price of experimentation. Put them where the uncertainty is.

Section 2.5 · Methodology

Choosing how to run the plan

Three ways to manage scope

Predictive, agile, or hybrid — matched to uncertainty

  • Predictive (waterfall) — fixed scope, linear phases. Best when requirements are stable and compliance is heavy (a regulated audit trail, a fixed contract).
  • Agile — flexible scope, incremental delivery in sprints. Best for the experimental heart of AI work, where you learn as you build.
  • Hybrid — fix the core scope, keep the experimentation areas iterative. Balances predictability with the freedom to discover — often the realistic choice for AI.

The rule of thumb: choose by uncertainty and risk. The more unknown the data and the model behaviour, the more agility you need; the more fixed the compliance and deadlines, the more structure.

Method follows uncertainty. Don't force a waterfall onto discovery, or improvise where an auditor needs a paper trail.

Keeping stakeholders aligned

Communicate the uncertainty, don't hide it

Whatever the method, the same discipline holds: identify business, technical and regulatory stakeholders early; present timelines with their buffers and iteration-dependent milestones; and run a change-control process so every scope change is a decision, not a surprise. A requirements traceability matrix — business objective → ML requirement → model output — keeps everyone honest about what's actually being built.

Section 1.1 · The hook

Your first AI project

Your decision

"When will it be done — and will it work?"

MekongPay's COO gives you six months, a data science team of five, and a clear brief: cut fraud losses with a machine-learning model. Then comes THE question every AI project manager dreads, because unlike normal software, nobody can promise a model will reach the accuracy the business needs: when will it be done, and will it work?

Before we answer, an instinct check: what share of AI projects fail — never delivering the value they promised?

Your estimate
50%
Locked. Research puts it around 80% — a 2024 RAND Corporation study found that over 80% of AI projects fail, roughly twice the failure rate of comparable non-AI IT projects. The causes are rarely the algorithms: they're unclear problems, bad data, and management approaches borrowed unchanged from traditional software. That gap — between how AI projects behave and how we manage them — is exactly what this course closes.
Why this course exists

An AI project delivers something no one can fully specify up front, built from data no one has fully seen, with an outcome no one can guarantee. None of that manages itself — and managing it like ordinary software is how you join the 80%.

AI projects don't fail because the model is bad. They fail because the project around the model was managed like it wasn't an AI project.

Section 1.2 · The definition

What is an AI project?

The definition

It learns from data — it isn't programmed rule by rule.

An AI project develops, trains, validates and deploys a system that learns patterns from data to make predictions or support decisions. Fraud detection, medical imaging, recommendation engines, predictive maintenance — different industries, same anatomy.

  • Traditional software is deterministic: same input, same output, behaviour written in rules.
  • AI systems are probabilistic: they output likelihoods — "87% probability this transaction is fraud" — learned from historical data.
  • That makes AI work experimental and iterative: you train, measure, refine — you cannot fully specify the result in advance.
The three core components

Data, algorithms, infrastructure — and data rules them all.

Every AI project stands on three legs: the data the model learns from, the algorithms that find the patterns, and the computing infrastructure that trains and serves the model. In practice the first leg decides your fate — a model is only as good as its data's quality (accurate, reliable), volume (enough to find real patterns) and diversity (covering the real world's scenarios). Poor or biased data produces confident, wrong, sometimes discriminatory predictions.

In AI projects, data is not an input to the plan. Data IS the plan.

Section 1.3 · The lifecycle

The five stages of an AI project

The framework

Every AI project moves through five stages — rarely in a straight line.

1 · Problem
define the business problem AI should solve
2 · Data
collect, clean and label the datasets
3 · Model
train algorithms to find the patterns
4 · Evaluation
measure accuracy, reliability, fairness
5 · Deployment
integrate, monitor, retrain in operations

Two things surprise first-time AI project managers. First, data work dominates — collecting, cleaning and labelling routinely eats more time than modelling itself. Second, the arrows point backwards as often as forwards: evaluation reveals data gaps, deployment reveals evaluation gaps, and the plan must absorb those loops rather than deny them.

And the lifecycle doesn't end at go-live: deployed models drift as the world changes — fraudsters adapt, customers change habits — so monitoring and retraining are part of the project design, not an afterthought.

Section 1.4 · The disciplines

Six knowledge areas, rewired for AI

The good news

Project management still applies. The bad news: every discipline bends.

AI initiatives are still managed through the classic knowledge areas — scope, time, cost, quality, risk, stakeholders (PMI's PMBOK). But each one behaves differently when the deliverable is a learning system:

  • Scope — evolves by design. A churn model may start with transaction history, then pull in support logs as insights emerge. Scope discipline means managing that evolution, not forbidding it.
  • Time — training cycles, tuning and validation make durations genuinely uncertain. A hospital's tumour-detection model may need dozens of training runs before accuracy is acceptable.
  • Cost — new cost drivers: data acquisition and labelling, scarce AI talent, and cloud compute that can triple during training.
  • Quality — beyond "does it work": accuracy, robustness, fairness and explainability. A hiring tool trained on biased history reproduces the bias, at scale.
  • Risk — new species: algorithmic bias, model drift, data privacy, regulation. A credit model that mirrors past discrimination is a legal and reputational time bomb.
  • Stakeholders — wider circle: engineers and executives, but also regulators and the people affected by automated decisions. Think of a city deploying facial recognition.
The trap

Managing the certainty you don't have

The classic failure: writing a traditional plan — fixed scope, fixed dates, fixed budget — over a process that is experimental at its core. When the first model underperforms (it usually does), the "plan" is already broken and the project starts living a double life. AI project management builds the iteration into the plan: staged commitments, experiment budgets, decision gates after each evaluation.

Plan the experiments, not just the outcome. A plan that can't survive a failed model was never a plan.

Section 1.5 · Success criteria

When is an AI project a success?

The core distinction

A brilliant model that changes nothing is a failed project.

Data scientists measure models in technical metrics — accuracy, precision, recall, latency. Organisations measure projects in business outcomes — fraud losses down, retention up, faster decisions. The two do not automatically connect: a recommendation model can improve its accuracy score and fail to sell a single extra product.

Between the model and the value sit two more layers the project manager owns:

  • Deployment — the model must survive contact with production: integration, real-time speed, monitoring, retraining pipelines. Models that shine on curated test data routinely stumble on messy live data.
  • Adoption — value only appears when people trust and use the system. If fraud analysts override the model's calls, or clinicians ignore the diagnostic aid, the project delivered nothing.

And increasingly a fifth dimension is non-negotiable: responsible AI — fairness, privacy, explainability, regulatory compliance (the NIST AI Risk Management Framework is the emerging reference). A credit model must satisfy fair-lending law, not just the ROC curve.

Mini case

Detecting diabetic retinopathy — what "success" took

Google's deep-learning model for diabetic retinopathy — a leading cause of preventable blindness — reached ophthalmologist-level accuracy, trained on over 100,000 retinal images (Gulshan et al., JAMA, 2016). A triumph? Technically, yes. But real-world success required the rest: massive high-quality labelled data, rigorous clinical validation, integration into screening workflows, and clinicians willing to act on its output. The model was the beginning of the project, not the end.

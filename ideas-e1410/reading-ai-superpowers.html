Why it's on this course. Every AI project you'll manage exists inside the story this book tells — the shift from an "age of discovery" to an age of implementation, where data and execution matter more than breakthrough research. Lee explains where the data, the talent and the capital are, why AI adoption is accelerating, and — crucially for a project manager — which human capabilities machines still can't touch.

The five things to remember
1
We've entered the "age of implementation," not discovery. The hard science (deep learning) is largely done and public. Advantage now goes to whoever has the most data and can execute fastest — not to whoever has the cleverest researchers. This is why project management, not just data science, decides who wins.
2
Data is the new oil, and China is the "Saudi Arabia of data." Because modern AI learns from examples, the sheer volume and richness of real-world data becomes the deciding resource — reframing every AI project as, first, a data project.
3
AI arrives in four waves. Internet AI, Business AI, Perception AI and Autonomous AI each unlock different applications and demand different projects. Knowing which wave your project rides tells you what data and risks to expect.
4
The real crisis is jobs and meaning, not killer robots. Lee dismisses the sci-fi "superintelligence" panic and focuses on displacement — and on which work stays human.
5
The human edge is compassion, creativity and connection. After a cancer diagnosis, Lee argues the future is human–AI symbiosis: let machines optimise, and reserve for people the empathy, judgement and care that AI cannot provide. For a PM, that's your job description.
Chapter map — the argument in six moves
Chapters 1–2 · China's Sputnik moment & the coliseum

How China became an AI superpower

AlphaGo's 2016 defeat of Go champion Lee Sedol was China's "Sputnik moment" — a national wake-up call that triggered enormous state and private investment. Lee argues China's brutally competitive ("gladiatorial") startup market, its copycat-turned-innovator entrepreneurs, and a techno-utilitarian government produced a uniquely fast AI ecosystem.

The question is no longer whether AI can change the world, but who will build it fastest — and on whose data.

Chapters 3–4 · Alternate internet & a tale of two countries

Why data volume beats algorithmic genius

China's mobile-first, cash-light "alternate internet universe" (super-apps, ubiquitous mobile payments, on-demand everything) generates staggering quantities of real-world behavioural data. In the age of implementation, Lee argues, that data advantage outweighs the U.S. lead in elite research — because today's AI improves mainly by seeing more examples, not by inventing new math.

PM lesson: before you scope an AI project, ask where the data will come from, who owns it, and whether it's rich enough. The answer often decides feasibility before a single model is trained.

Chapter 5 · The Four Waves of AI

The core framework — four waves, four kinds of project

  • Internet AI — recommendation engines learning from your clicks (news feeds, e-commerce, streaming). Data is already labelled by user behaviour.
  • Business AI — algorithms mining an organisation's historical records (credit scoring, fraud, diagnosis support). Depends on decades of structured, labelled data.
  • Perception AI — digitising the physical world through sensors, vision and speech (smart speakers, cashier-less stores, face recognition). Blurs online and offline.
  • Autonomous AI — machines that sense and act in the world (self-driving cars, autonomous drones, industrial robots). Highest stakes, highest safety burden.

Each wave is a different data problem, a different risk profile, and a different project. Name your wave and you know what you're managing.

Chapter 6 · Utopia, dystopia & the real AI crisis

The honest reckoning with jobs

Lee sets aside superintelligence fears as a distraction and confronts the near-term disruption: AI will displace large shares of routine cognitive and manual work. He maps jobs on two axes — compassion and creativity/strategy — to show which roles are safe, which are exposed, and which become human–machine collaborations.

Chapters 7–9 · The wisdom of cancer & a blueprint

The human answer

A lymphoma diagnosis reframed Lee's own thinking: an optimising machine has no purpose of its own; meaning comes from human love and connection. His blueprint rejects a pure cash fix (universal basic income) in favour of a social investment stipend rewarding care, community and creativity — and a future of human–AI symbiosis where AI handles optimisation and humans provide compassion and judgement.

Let the machine do what it does best — crunch, optimise, predict. Reserve for people what only people can do — care, connect, decide with wisdom.

What you're looking at

The 1801 census counted heads but botched occupations: it sorted households into just “Agriculture”, “Trade” or “Other”, and for most counties those three sum to about half the population — leaving us to guess what everyone else did. The first usable occupational census is 1851, by which point the first wave of industrialisation was already over.

To see the wave itself, Brunt and Meidell built a synthetic occupational census for 1801. They drew a stratified sample of roughly 100,000 businesses from the Universal British Directory (1793–98), reflated each trade by its 1851 employees-per-business, and added the farm sector (from Arthur Young's survey of 400 farms), the government, the military and the fishery from contemporary returns. Every worker is classified into the 369 occupations and 17 classes of the 1851 census.

The Professions view shows that 1801 census directly, county by county: 353 occupations, each number reconstructed from the directories. The figures here match the published study to the head — 192,969 in flax and linen, 177,667 weavers, 22,626 making woollen cloth in Devonshire alone. The Sectors view steps back to the primary / secondary / tertiary shares and adds 1851, so you can watch land empty out while industry and services swell.

Nationally the industrial share of employment rose from about 31% to 41% and agriculture fell from 38% to 28% — but the averages hide the real story, which is regional. Lancashire's primary sector halved while its secondary sector ran to nearly 60% of all work; Monmouthshire and South Wales swung violently into coal and iron; the counties ringing London specialised into farming to feed the capital.

Section 3.1 · The Hook

Who decides what "sustainable" means?

Your decision

Marketing wants to call the new fund "sustainable".

Léman is launching a low-carbon European equity fund. The marketing team has already designed the brochure — "Léman Sustainable Europe" — and wants to sell it to a large German pension client. The CEO stops the room: "Before that word goes on a single page — who actually decides whether we're allowed to call it 'sustainable'? Us? Bern? Brussels?"

Five years ago the honest answer was "whoever writes the brochure." That era is over. Commit first: which authority now has the final say over that one word on this fund?

Place your bet — then lock it
Pick the one authority you'd bet on.
Locked. Here's the catch: the honest answer is all of them at once. The moment Léman markets that fund into the EU, SFDR reaches across the border and dictates what it must disclose and which "green" claims it may make — Switzerland is a rule-taker for EU market access. At home, FINMA and the AMAS self-regulation already forbid calling a product "sustainable", "green" or "ESG" without substance. And neither book lets you self-define the word any more: the Taxonomy says which activities are green, CSRD forces the underlying data, and SFDR governs the fund label. This week is the anatomy of those four rulebooks — and by section 3.5 you'll see exactly where Léman is caught by both.
One claim, four gatekeepers

The word "sustainable" now passes four checkpoints.

A modern sustainability claim is only as strong as the weakest rule behind it. Follow the claim down through the stack:

Each checkpoint answers a different question, and they hand off to one another: the Taxonomy defines green, CSRD measures it at company level, SFDR governs the product claim, and the Swiss regime decides what Léman must do as a Geneva firm. Miss any one and the claim is exposed.

Why this week exists

Week 2 said the loop only closes when policy defines "green", firms disclose, and data becomes comparable. This week is those three blocks made law — plus the Swiss layer Léman actually lives under.

Get the rulebook wrong and the cost isn't abstract: a mislabelled fund is a greenwashing case, a regulatory finding, and a headline (remember DWS in Week 1). Get it right and the same rules become Léman's edge — because most competitors are still treating them as a compliance chore.

In 2020, "sustainable" was a marketing word. Today it is a regulated claim with four gatekeepers — and a Swiss firm answers to two capitals.

Section 3.2 · The Classifier

The EU Taxonomy — what counts as green

Three tests, six objectives

The dictionary the whole system points at.

The EU Taxonomy (Regulation 2020/852) is the classification system that answers "is this activity environmentally sustainable?" — so every other rule has something concrete to point at. It sets six environmental objectives:

  • climate change mitigation; and adaptation;
  • sustainable use of water & marine resources;
  • transition to a circular economy;
  • pollution prevention & control;
  • protection of biodiversity & ecosystems.

To count as aligned, an economic activity must clear all three tests:

  • Substantial contribution to at least one objective (meeting a technical screening threshold);
  • Do No Significant Harm (DNSH) to the other five; and
  • Minimum safeguards — the OECD Guidelines and the UN Guiding Principles on Business & Human Rights.

The distinction the exam loves

Eligible ≠ aligned.

This is where most people trip. Two different words:

  • Eligible — the activity is described by the Taxonomy (it's one of the activities the rules cover, e.g. cement manufacturing). Being eligible says nothing about performance.
  • Aligned — the activity is eligible and actually passes substantial contribution + DNSH + safeguards.

So a coal-heavy cement plant can be eligible (cement is a covered activity) yet nowhere near aligned. A company reports both: what share of its Turnover, CapEx and OpEx is eligible, and what share is aligned. Investors mostly care about the aligned number — the eligible figure is just the denominator of the conversation.

"Eligible" means the taxonomy has an opinion about it. "Aligned" means it passed.

Section 3.3 · The Product Rule

SFDR — what a fund may call itself

Article 6, 8, 9

Three drawers every EU fund is sorted into.

The SFDR (Regulation 2019/2088) makes managers disclose how sustainable their products are. In practice the market reads it as three tiers:

  • Article 6 — no sustainability claim; the manager just discloses how it handles sustainability risk. The baseline.
  • Article 8 ("light green") — the product promotes environmental or social characteristics (e.g. a low-carbon tilt), but sustainability isn't its objective.
  • Article 9 ("dark green") — sustainable investment is the objective; in practice held to almost 100% sustainable investments.

Managers also report Principal Adverse Impacts (PAIs) — the harm their investments do on standard indicators like carbon, biodiversity and board diversity.

The mislabel at the heart of the market

SFDR is a disclosure regime — not a labelling regime.

"Article 8" and "Article 9" were never meant to be quality labels. They are disclosure categories. But the market turned them into de-facto badges — and then reality bit.

When regulators clarified that Article 9 means essentially 100% sustainable investments, a wave of funds that had claimed the top tier were downgraded from Article 9 to Article 8 in late 2022 and 2023 — hundreds of products, hundreds of billions in assets, re-badged almost overnight. The lesson for Léman: choosing the drawer is a disclosure commitment you must live up to, not a marketing choice. Over-claim and you'll be re-classifying under a headline.

This is exactly why the Commission has been reviewing SFDR — to move it toward clearer categories, closer to real labels.

Section 3.4 · The Data Layer

CSRD & ESRS — the data underneath

The plumbing for everything else

No disclosure, no Taxonomy, no SFDR.

The Taxonomy and SFDR only work if the underlying company data exists. That's the job of the CSRD (Directive 2022/2464), which replaced the older NFRD and hugely widens who must report — using a single rulebook, the ESRS standards (twelve standards: two cross-cutting, five environmental, four social, one governance).

CSRD is the fix for the "information gap" market failure from Week 2: it forces comparable, assured sustainability data — starting with limited assurance — so capital can finally tell a leader from a laggard.

Double materiality

Two directions of "material", not one.

CSRD's signature idea is double materiality. A sustainability matter must be reported if it is material in either of two directions:

  • Impact materiality (inside-out) — the company's effect on people and the planet (its emissions, its supply-chain labour).
  • Financial materiality (outside-in) — sustainability's effect on the company's own value (carbon costs, physical climate risk to its assets).

Traditional accounting only ever asked the second question. CSRD asks both — and many issues turn out to be both at once, which is exactly the point.

Moving target — read the news, not just the textbook

The 2025 "Omnibus" is rewriting the scope.

CSRD's original phase-in reached companies in waves — the largest first, then other large companies, then listed SMEs, plus large non-EU groups. But in 2025 the EU's Omnibus simplification package hit the brakes: a "stop-the-clock" law delayed the later waves by two years, and the Commission proposed raising the size threshold sharply (toward ~1,000 employees), which would cut the number of companies in scope dramatically.

For the exam, know the architecture — replace NFRD, ESRS, double materiality, phased scope, assurance. For the real world, know that the who and when are being renegotiated. When you quote a scope date in your assignment, date your source.

Section 3.5 · Home Ground

The Swiss regime — where Léman lives

A different philosophy

Principles & self-regulation, not a Swiss taxonomy.

Switzerland deliberately did not copy the EU's prescriptive rulebook. There is no Swiss taxonomy and no Swiss SFDR. Instead the approach is principles-based and leans on industry self-regulation:

  • FINMA Guidance on greenwashing — the supervisor applies existing law so clients aren't misled about a product's sustainability.
  • Self-regulation — in 2024 the Federal Council chose to let the industry (AMAS for asset managers, the Swiss Bankers Association for banks) self-regulate rather than legislate. AMAS's rules already bar calling a product "sustainable", "green" or "ESG" without substance.
  • Swiss Climate Scores — a voluntary best-practice set of indicators (introduced 2022) for showing how Paris-aligned a portfolio is.
  • Mandatory climate reporting — large Swiss companies must publish TCFD-aligned climate disclosures (Ordinance on Climate Disclosures, in force since 2024).

The rule-taker reality

The lighter touch at home doesn't set you free.

It's tempting to read Switzerland's principles-based regime as "easier". For a Geneva firm with EU ambitions, it isn't. The instant Léman markets a fund into the EU — that German pension client from section 3.1 — the full EU stack applies: SFDR disclosures, Taxonomy alignment figures, PAIs. Switzerland is effectively a rule-taker: to sell into the single market, you meet the single market's rules. So Léman runs to the higher of the two bars, not the lower.

Bern sets the floor at home; Brussels sets the ceiling the moment you cross the border. Léman must clear the ceiling.

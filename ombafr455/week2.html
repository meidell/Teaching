Section 2.1 · The Hook

Who moves the money?

Your decision

The board wants one lever to bet on.

The foundation mandate from Week 1 is signed. Now the CEO of Léman Sustainable Capital pulls you into a strategy session: "The rules are changing fast. If we had to bet the firm's positioning on the one force that will actually redirect capital toward sustainability, which is it?"

The room is split — some say it's all about regulation, others swear the real power sits with the big pension funds, or the banks, or even individual savers. Before we settle it, commit. Place your bet on the single most powerful lever.

Place your bet — then lock it
Pick the one lever you'd bet on.
Locked. Here's the catch: whichever you picked is right and wrong at once. No single actor can green the system alone — a regulator's taxonomy is useless without companies disclosing data; an asset owner's mandate is empty without products to buy; a bank's green loan needs a rule that defines "green". The answer is a system: interlocking actors, information and rules that only work together. This whole week is about how those pieces fit — and by section 2.5 you'll see exactly what breaks when one is missing.
Three levels of the same game

Product → institution → system.

"Sustainable finance" is used at three zoom levels, and mixing them up is a classic source of confusion:

  • The product. A single green bond or fund — is it credible? (That was Week 1.)
  • The institution. A whole bank, insurer or asset manager aligning its book, its risk management and its lending. (That's your job at Léman.)
  • The system. The actors, information and rules that make sustainable allocation the default across the whole economy. (That's this week — and the reason regulation exists.)

Each level nests inside the next. A credible product inside an unreformed system is a drop in a rising tide; a reformed system makes credible products the easy choice.

Why this week exists

Notice the trustee's suspicion from last week — the loudest claim is rarely the best-evidenced one. The same is true here: the loudest lever is rarely the whole answer.

To move real money at scale, you have to change the system the money flows through: rewire the actors, the information and the rules so capital systematically flows to sustainable outcomes. This week is the map of that rewiring — and the map of the regulation (Week 3) that enforces it.

One green fund is a choice. A green system is a set of rules that make the choice the default.

Section 2.2 · The Rationale

Why the market won't green itself

Four market failures

If sustainability paid off cleanly, no policy would be needed.

Left alone, markets systematically under-invest in sustainability. Not because investors are wicked — because four well-understood market failures get in the way:

  • Negative externalities. A polluter imposes costs on society (dirty air, a heating planet) that it never pays for. So "brown" activities look artificially cheap and profitable.
  • The tragedy of the horizon. The worst climate costs land decades out — beyond the horizon of the CEO, the fund manager, even the political cycle. What lies past the horizon barely dents today's prices.
  • Information gaps. Without comparable, reliable data, capital cannot tell a genuine leader from a laggard — so it can't reward the leader.
  • Misaligned incentives. Quarterly targets, annual bonuses and benchmark-hugging mandates all reward the short term, punishing anyone who invests for a payoff years away.

Each of these is a reason the market can't self-correct — and therefore a reason a system has to be deliberately built.

The three climate risks

Why climate became a financial-stability problem, not just an ethical one.

The breakthrough insight of the last decade is that sustainability isn't only about values — it is financial risk. Mark Carney's framing names three channels:

  • Physical risk — the direct hit from a changing climate: floods, storms, droughts, wildfires damaging assets and disrupting supply chains.
  • Transition risk — losses from the shift to a low-carbon economy: policy, carbon prices, technology and shifting demand that can strand fossil assets overnight.
  • Liability risk — claims against those who caused, or failed to manage, climate damage — flowing back to insurers and the firms they cover.

Once a supervisor accepts these are real, climate stops being a CSR footnote and becomes something a central bank must stress-test. That is exactly why the actors in section 2.3 now include central banks.

Section 2.3 · The Actors

Who's who — and how the money flows

Capital passes through many hands on its way to the real economy.

Around this chain sit the enablers: banks (a parallel channel — deposits into loans), regulators (the rules), central banks & supervisors (stability), standard-setters (how to report), data & ratings providers (the referees), and stock exchanges (the marketplace).

The full cast

Everyone with a hand on the money.

Beyond the core chain, a working system needs each of these — and a gap in any one lets sustainability leak away:

  • Asset owners — pension funds, insurers, sovereign & endowment funds. They own the capital and set the mandate.
  • Asset managers — invest the owners' money to the mandate; the industry Léman competes in.
  • Banks — turn deposits into loans; green & sustainability-linked lending is their lever.
  • Companies / issuers — the real-economy actors whose behaviour the whole system is trying to change.
  • Regulators & policymakers — write the taxonomy, disclosure duties and fund labels.
  • Central banks & supervisors — guard stability; stress-test climate risk (the NGFS crowd).
  • Standard-setters — the ISSB, GRI and others: define how sustainability is reported so data is comparable.
  • Data & ratings providers — the "referees" who score companies; powerful, and often disagreeing with each other.
  • Auditors / assurance providers — check that sustainability reports are true, turning claims into evidence.
  • Stock exchanges & index providers — list issuers and build the benchmarks that quietly steer trillions.
  • NGOs & civil society — the watchdogs who expose greenwashing (recall DWS in Week 1).

Where sustainability leaks out of the chain

Every hop is a principal–agent problem.

Each time money passes from one actor to the next, the second acts on behalf of the first — and their interests don't perfectly align. The saver wants a liveable retirement and planet; the manager wants to beat a benchmark this year; the company wants the cheapest capital. Sustainability intentions get diluted at each handoff unless the mandate, the data and the rules carry them through.

This is why the asset owner's mandate matters so much: it's the instruction that has to survive all the way down the chain — and increasingly, fiduciary duty is read to require owners to consider material ESG risk, not merely permit it.

A green intention at the top means nothing if it's lost at the third handoff.

Section 2.4 · The Rulebook

The rules being built

The EU Sustainable Finance Action Plan

One plan, three objectives — the blueprint everyone else now references.

In 2018 the European Commission adopted its Sustainable Finance Action Plan — the most influential attempt yet to build a sustainable financial system. Its ten actions serve three objectives:

  • 1 · Reorient capital flows toward sustainable investment — chiefly by defining what "sustainable" means (the taxonomy) and building green benchmarks.
  • 2 · Mainstream sustainability into risk management — make firms and supervisors treat climate and ESG as financial risk.
  • 3 · Foster transparency and long-termism — force comparable disclosure (SFDR for funds, CSRD for companies) and lengthen decision horizons.

Every EU rule you'll meet in Week 3 hangs off one of these three hooks. Learn the hooks now and the detail later fits into place.

Inside the taxonomy

Three tests an activity must pass to be "green".

The EU Taxonomy defines six environmental objectives: climate change mitigation; climate change adaptation; water & marine resources; the circular economy; pollution prevention; and biodiversity & ecosystems. To count as sustainable, an activity must:

  • Substantially contribute to at least one of the six objectives;
  • Do no significant harm (DNSH) to any of the other five; and
  • Meet minimum social safeguards (human-rights and labour standards).

It's demanding by design: the whole point is to make "green" a claim you can audit, not a colour on a brochure. You'll work through it in detail in Week 3 — here, just hold the shape.

Section 2.5 · The System Loop

Make it self-reinforcing

The scale of the task

Why the system has to mobilise private capital.

UNCTAD estimates the developing world alone faces an annual SDG investment gap of about US$4 trillion — up roughly 60% from the US$2.5 trillion estimated in 2015. Government budgets and aid cannot come close to closing it. Only if the financial system is rewired to route mainstream private capital toward sustainable outcomes does the arithmetic work.

That is the real stake behind objective #1, "reorient capital flows": not a nudge, but the mobilisation of trillions a year. You'll compute the gap yourself in the homework.

A loop, not a list

The building blocks only work together.

A sustainable financial system is a loop: policy defines what's green (taxonomy) → firms must disclose → data becomes comparable → capital flows to genuine leaders → the real economy shifts → the evidence feeds back into better policy. Pull one block out and the whole loop stalls. Try it.

Remove a building block
The loop turns.
Everything in place — capital finds the leaders, and the real economy shifts.

This is why "just launch a green fund" isn't enough, and why the A4S report Financing our Future frames the task as system-building: the finance function, standard-setters and policymakers each have to move, or the loop never closes.

The finance function has to move

The A4S message to finance leaders is blunt: sustainability has to be built into mainstream decisions — treasury, risk, capital allocation — not bolted on as a side project. That is exactly the shift Léman is positioning for, and the reason your board briefing matters. The firms that treat this as a compliance chore will be the ones caught flat-footed when the rules bite in Week 3.

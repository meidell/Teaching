Your decision

Why this course exists

Notice the trustee's suspicion — "just a marketing sticker" — is half right. For years, "sustainable" could be printed on a fund cover with almost nothing behind it. That era is ending: regulators, clients and courts now demand substance.

Sustainable finance is the discipline of putting substance behind the label: knowing exactly what a strategy does, why, and being able to show the receipts. Every topic in the next ten weeks — debt, equity, derivatives, carbon markets — serves one purpose: sustainability claims you can defend.

A sustainability claim you can defend beats one you can only advertise.

Section 1.2 · The Definition

What sustainable investing is

The core definition

One lens wider than ordinary investing.

Sustainable investing is an approach that considers environmental, social and governance (ESG) factors — alongside financial return — in the selection and management of investments. Related labels (responsible investing, ESG investing, SRI, impact investing) are points on a spectrum, not separate worlds.

Sustainable investing = financial return + ESG factors + (at the far end) intended, measurable real-world impact

The common myth is that it means sacrificing return. The accurate framing: it widens the information set a serious investor uses — and, at the impact end, adds a deliberate real-world goal.

The distinction that runs through everything

Two very different reasons to do it.

Almost every sustainable-investing decision is driven by one of two motives — and confusing them is the most common error in this field:

  • Financial-materiality (risk / return). You account for ESG because it affects the money. A coal miner faces stranded-asset risk; a factory with weak governance faces fines. This is the logic of ESG integration — sharper analysis, same goal: return.
  • Values / real-world impact. You account for ESG because of the outcome in the world, and you'd do it even at a cost — excluding tobacco on principle, or funding renewable capacity you can measure. This is the logic of exclusions on ethical grounds and of impact investing.

Many strategies blend both. But when a client, a regulator or an exam question asks "why?", you must be able to say which motive is doing the work — because it decides what evidence counts as success.

Section 1.3 · The Toolkit

Know your strategy

The spectrum of capital

Every approach sits on one line — from "avoid harm" to "impact-first".

The spectrum of capital orders approaches by intent and by the role of financial return. Move left to right and the goal shifts from "do no harm" to "actively contribute to solutions" — and the willingness to trade return for outcome rises.

Financial-onlyreturn is the sole goal
Responsibleavoid ESG harm & risk
Sustainabletilt toward ESG leaders
Impactintended, measured outcomes
Philanthropyoutcome first, return optional

Note where the instruments of this course land: an ESG-integrated equity fund sits around "responsible/sustainable"; a green bond that finances measurable renewable capacity (your written assignment on Apple) reaches toward "impact".

The seven strategies

"Sustainable investing" is an umbrella over seven distinct tools.

The GSIA and SSF group the field into seven strategies. You should be able to name each and recognise it in the wild:

  • Exclusionary screening — remove sectors/firms (tobacco, controversial weapons, thermal coal). The oldest strategy.
  • Norms-based screening — drop firms breaching minimum international norms (UN Global Compact, OECD, ILO).
  • ESG integration — fold material ESG factors into mainstream analysis and valuation. Now the largest by assets.
  • Best-in-class / positive screening — keep the ESG leaders within each sector.
  • Sustainability-themed investing — build a portfolio around a theme (clean water, renewable energy).
  • Impact investing — invest with the intention to create, and measure, a real-world outcome.
  • Corporate engagement & shareholder action — use ownership rights (voting, resolutions) to change behaviour: stewardship.

The backbone

The PRI: the field's shared starting point.

The Principles for Responsible Investment (PRI) — UN-supported, launched in 2006 — are the backbone of the field. Signatories voluntarily commit to six principles: incorporate ESG into analysis; be active owners; seek ESG disclosure from investees; promote the principles; work together; and report on progress.

Signatories now represent the majority of global assets under management. When someone says "responsible investor", the PRI is the framework they're measured against.

Section 1.4 · Credibility

Credible, or greenwashing?

The problem the trustee spotted

As the market grew, so did the label without the substance.

A product can print "sustainable" on the cover and do almost none of the work behind it. That's greenwashing — and regulators, clients and courts are now far less forgiving of it.

Swiss Sustainable Finance and the Asset Management Association Switzerland (AMAS) set out what a credible product looks like — and it reduces to three questions you can ask of anything on your desk:

  • What does it do? A defined sustainable objective, not just a label.
  • How does it do it? A transparent method — named screens, exclusions, engagement.
  • Where are the receipts? Regular, comparable reporting on outcomes, not intentions.

A credible product can tell you what it does, how, and shows you the receipts. Greenwashing can't.

Three ways a "sustainable" fund fools you
  • Label without objective — "Sustainable" on the cover, but no stated sustainable goal in the prospectus. A sticker, not a strategy.
  • Method you can't inspect — "net-zero aligned", "ESG-optimised" — slogans with no disclosed screens, exclusions or methodology behind them.
  • No receipts — no regular, comparable reporting on what actually happened. Intentions are cheap; outcomes are the evidence.

None of these are visible from the fund name — the flaw lives in the documentation, so that's where the check must happen.

Case file · when it goes wrong

DWS: a €1 trillion manager caught overstating "ESG".

In 2021, DWS — Deutsche Bank's asset-management arm — was accused by its own former head of sustainability, Desiree Fixler, of overstating in its annual report how much of its assets were genuinely run with ESG integration. The fallout was not a slap on the wrist:

  • German authorities raided DWS offices in 2022; the CEO resigned days later.
  • In 2023, DWS agreed to pay the US SEC US$19 million — a landmark greenwashing penalty against an asset manager — over misleading statements about its ESG controls.

The lesson for you at Léman: an ESG claim is now a regulated statement, not marketing copy. If you can't show the what, the how and the receipts, don't make the claim.

Section 1.5 · The Market

How big is it, really?

The number depends on the ruler

One market. Four defensible numbers.

Ask "how big is sustainable investing?" and you'll be quoted anything from a couple of trillion dollars to sixty-plus. Nobody is lying — the figure moves entirely with which strategies you agree to count. Slide the definition from strict to loose and watch the headline move.

Choose a definition
US$30 trn

This is why you must ask what a figure counts before you trust it — and why strategies overlap (a fund can use several at once), so you can never simply add them up.

The dip that isn't what it looks like

The global headline fell — while the market grew.

The GSIA headline fell from roughly US$35 trillion in 2020 to about US$30 trillion in 2022. Read that carefully: it was driven mainly by the US restating sharply downward as definitions tightened under greenwashing scrutiny — not by investors fleeing. Markets outside the US actually grew over the same period.

The Swiss lens

Switzerland is among the world's leading hubs for sustainable finance. Swiss Sustainable Finance's annual market study tracks volumes well into the trillions of francs across funds, mandates and asset-owner portfolios. Because the figure — and the method behind it — moves each year, treat the study itself as the live source rather than memorising a single number. It's the reading that grounds your Léman mandate in the Swiss market you actually operate in.

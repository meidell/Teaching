Brunt & García-Peñalosa · Europe · 1500–1900

The Engine
in the Crowd

Textbooks say growth builds cities: get richer, and people move to town. This paper turns the arrow around. Crowd people together first, and the density itself — the contact, the learning, the copying — manufactures the growth. Scroll to watch the loop close.

After Brunt & García-Peñalosa, “Urbanisation and the Onset of Modern Economic Growth,” The Economic Journal 132(642), 2022. The paper is a model; the paths and city sizes here are a synthetic stand-in illustrating its dynamics.

Centuries flat, then the hook

The signature of the model is a hockey stick: urbanisation and output per worker barely move for two hundred years, then bend sharply upward once the feedback between density and productivity catches. The vertical line marks the exogenous farm-productivity shock that lets the first workers leave the land.

About this build. The causal loop reproduces the paper’s mechanism exactly; the Europe map and the hockey-stick chart use synthetic series shaped to match its qualitative claims (flat pre-industrial paths, a post-shock take-off, agglomeration concentrating in Britain and the Low Countries first). City populations are illustrative round figures, not the paper’s estimates.
This page visualises a model, so there is no dataset to swap in — but the synthetic paths in make_urb.py can be replaced with digitised points from the paper’s figures to track its actual curves.

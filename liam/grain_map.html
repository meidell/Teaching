The London Gazette · weekly corn returns · 1771–1820

One Market,
Slowly Made

For fifty years the British state printed the price of grain in every county, week after week. Watch those forty-odd prices drift apart in war and dearth — and, across half a century of better roads, banks and newspapers, quietly converge into a single national market.

Data structure after Brunt & Cannon, “Weekly British Grain Prices from the London Gazette, 1770–1820” (UK Data Service SN 4383).
Figures shown are a synthetic stand-in matched to that format — pence per bushel, by county, missing = −1 — pending the licensed file.

About this build. The map is a stylised engraving of the historic counties, not a survey-accurate boundary file — it sidesteps the mismatch between 1771 counties and modern GIS borders. The parser already follows the real SN 4383 rules: prices in pence per bushel, the −1 missing code, and the leap-year-ignoring day-of-year date. London reports only to 1795; Wales appears as North/South before 1790, then as twelve counties.

To use the genuine data: register with the UK Data Service, download SN 4383, and replace the synthetic CSV — the aggregation and page consume the same columns unchanged.
Replace grain_4383.csv with the licensed file, re-run aggregate.py, and reload. No other change is needed.

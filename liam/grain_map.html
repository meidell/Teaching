Reading the returns

The return is the estimated gross rate of return to growing an acre of wheat: roughly, what the crop fetched against what it cost to put in the ground. It swings violently year to year because both halves move — a failed harvest lifts the price of what survives, while a glut collapses it.

Two features dominate the half-century. First, the great dearths: the mid-1790s and especially 1795 and 1800, when scarcity drove returns above 60% almost everywhere. Second, the collapse after Waterloo — 1814 to 1816 — when peace, good weather and cheap imports turned wheat into a loss across most of England and Wales.

Underneath the common shocks lies a quieter regional story. Over the long run the northern counties — Durham, Northumberland, the North Riding — and the Welsh borders earned consistently higher returns than the arable south and east, whose returns were lower and steadier.

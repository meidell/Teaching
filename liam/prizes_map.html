For a century the RASE travelled England, staging a contest of machines at a different town each year and handing out medals for the best. Did the prizes actually cause invention — or merely decorate it? Scroll to follow the shows across the map, and watch patents spike in the year each prize was contested.

After Brunt, Lerner & Nicholas, “Inducement Prizes and Innovation,” Journal of Industrial Economics 60(4), 2012. No public dataset exists; figures here are a synthetic stand-in calibrated to the paper’s headline numbers.

One line, one claim

Plot patenting against the show calendar and the paper’s argument shows up as rhythm: invention rises in the years a prize is on the table. The shaded band marks the strict rotation scheme of 1856–1872, when categories were fixed years ahead — so the surge can’t simply be the Society chasing whatever was already hot.

About this build. The map is a stylised engraving of England & Wales; show towns are placed at real coordinates and visited in an illustrative order. Totals match the paper (≈15,032 entries, ≈1,986 awards, 13 of 16 gold medals, Manchester 1897 the best-attended show at 217,980), but the year-by-year series is synthetic, since no replication file was published.
To use real figures, replace the generated series in make_prizes.py with values transcribed from the paper’s tables and re-run. The page reads the same fields unchanged.

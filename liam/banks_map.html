The Country Banks

Outside London, Britain's industrial economy ran on hundreds of small provincial banks — private partnerships of unlimited liability, lending to the mills, farms and merchants around them. Drag through the years to watch the network swell to its 1825 peak, crash in the panic that December, and then give way to a new species: the joint-stock bank, legalised in 1826.

The rise, the crash, the joint-stock bank

A country bank was rarely more than a partnership of a few local men — a corn merchant, a brewer, an attorney — issuing their own notes and discounting the bills of nearby trade. With Bank of England notes scarce in the provinces, these houses were the everyday money of industrialising Britain. Their number climbed from 370 in 1800 to a peak of 684 in 1825.

Then came the panic. In December 1825 a speculative bubble burst, a run swept the provinces, and dozens of country banks failed in weeks — the total fell to 597 by 1826. The fragility of the small partnership had been laid bare.

Parliament's answer was the 1826 Banking Act, which allowed joint-stock banks — with many shareholders and far deeper capital — anywhere more than 65 miles from London. Switch to joint-stock and run the years forward: from 3 banks in 1826 they reach 590 by 1844, concentrated in exactly the industrial counties — Lancashire, the West Riding, Warwickshire — where the old private banks had been busiest. The provincial financial system was being rebuilt on sturdier foundations.
